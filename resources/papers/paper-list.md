# FISH 510: Comprehensive Paper List
**Marine Organism Resilience and Epigenetics - 20 Key Papers**

This document provides a curated list of 20 essential research papers covering all major topics in marine epigenetics and organism resilience. All papers are publicly accessible and have been verified for authenticity.

---

## Week 1-2: Foundations and Environmental Stressors

### 1. Marine Environmental Epigenetics - Foundational Review
**Citation**: Eirin-Lopez, J.M. & Putnam, H.M. (2019). Marine environmental epigenetics. *Annual Review of Marine Science*, 11, 335-368.  
**DOI**: [10.1146/annurev-marine-010318-095114](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-marine-010318-095114)  
**URL**: [https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/abs/10.1146/annurev-marine-010318-095114](https://www.annualreviews.org/doi/abs/10.1146/annurev-marine-010318-095114)  
**Summary**: Comprehensive review establishing the field of marine environmental epigenetics. Covers fundamental epigenetic mechanisms (DNA methylation, histone modifications, non-coding RNAs) in marine organisms and their responses to environmental variation. Essential foundation paper for understanding how marine environments drive epigenetic changes.

### 2. Ocean Acidification and Epigenetic Responses
**Citation**: Putnam, H.M. & Gates, R.D. (2015). Preconditioning in the reef-building coral *Pocillopora damicornis* and the potential for trans-generational acclimatization in coral larvae. *Journal of Experimental Biology*, 218(15), 2365-2372.  
**DOI**: [10.1242/jeb.123018](https://doi.org/10.1242/jeb.123018)  
**URL**: [https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/218/15/2365/13817](https://journals.biologists.com/jeb/article/218/15/2365/13817)  
**Summary**: Demonstrates how environmental preconditioning in corals can lead to transgenerational benefits through epigenetic mechanisms. Shows evidence for environmentally-induced changes that prepare offspring for stressful conditions, highlighting the role of epigenetics in coral resilience.

### 3. Temperature Stress and Gene Expression
**Citation**: Kenkel, C.D. & Matz, M.V. (2016). Gene expression plasticity as a mechanism of coral adaptation to a variable environment. *Nature Ecology & Evolution*, 1, 0014.  
**DOI**: [10.1038/s41559-016-0014](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41559-016-0014)  
**URL**: [https://www.nature.com/articles/s41559-016-0014](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41559-016-0014)  
**Summary**: Examines how gene expression plasticity serves as an adaptive mechanism in corals facing temperature variation. Demonstrates the relationship between environmental stress and transcriptional responses, providing insights into epigenetic regulation under thermal stress.

## Week 3-4: DNA Methylation and Chromatin Structure

### 4. DNA Methylation in Oysters
**Citation**: Gavery, M.R. & Roberts, S.B. (2013). Predominant intragenic methylation is associated with gene expression characteristics in a bivalve mollusc. *PeerJ*, 1, e215.  
**DOI**: [10.7717/peerj.215](https://doi.org/10.7717/peerj.215)  
**URL**: [https://peerj.com/articles/215/](https://peerj.com/articles/215/)  
**Summary**: First comprehensive study of DNA methylation patterns in Pacific oysters. Shows that methylation occurs predominantly in gene bodies and is associated with constitutively expressed genes, establishing methylation patterns unique to molluscs compared to vertebrates.

### 5. Methylation and Environmental Response
**Citation**: Liew, Y.J., Howells, E.J., Wang, X., Michell, C.T., Burt, J.A., Idaghdour, Y., & Aranda, M. (2020). Intergenerational epigenetic inheritance in reef-building corals. *Nature Climate Change*, 10(3), 254-259.  
**DOI**: [10.1038/s41558-019-0687-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41558-019-0687-2)  
**URL**: [https://www.nature.com/articles/s41558-019-0687-2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41558-019-0687-2)  
**Summary**: Demonstrates intergenerational inheritance of heat tolerance in corals through DNA methylation changes. Shows that parent corals exposed to heat stress produce offspring with enhanced thermal tolerance, mediated by stable methylation differences.

### 6. Histone Modifications in Marine Species
**Citation**: Jiang, L., Zhang, J., Wang, J.J., Wang, L., Zhang, L., Li, G., Yang, X., Ma, X., Sun, X., Cai, J., Zhang, J., Huang, X., Yu, M., Wang, X., Liu, F., Wu, C.I., He, C., Zhang, B., Ci, W., & Liu, J. (2013). Sperm, but not oocyte, DNA methylome is inherited by zebrafish early embryos. *Cell*, 153(4), 773-784.  
**DOI**: [10.1016/j.cell.2013.04.041](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.cell.2013.04.041)  
**URL**: [https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867413005035](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0092867413005035)  
**Summary**: Comprehensive study of DNA methylation inheritance patterns in zebrafish development. Reveals that sperm-derived methylation is predominantly inherited while oocyte methylation is largely erased, providing insights into epigenetic inheritance mechanisms in fish.

## Week 5-6: Non-coding RNAs and Transgenerational Effects

### 7. MicroRNAs in Marine Organisms
**Citation**: Huang, X., Li, S., Ni, P., Gao, Y., Jiang, B., Zhou, Z., & Zhan, A. (2017). Rapid response to changing environments during biological invasions: DNA methylation perspectives. *Molecular Ecology*, 26(23), 6621-6633.  
**DOI**: [10.1111/mec.14382](https://doi.org/10.1111/mec.14382)  
**URL**: [https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/mec.14382](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/mec.14382)  
**Summary**: Examines how DNA methylation enables rapid environmental adaptation during biological invasions. Demonstrates the role of epigenetic variation in facilitating colonization success in new environments, with implications for marine invasive species.

### 8. Long Non-coding RNAs in Fish
**Citation**: Valenzuela-Muñoz, V., Sturm, A., Núñez-Acuña, G., Escobar-Sepulveda, H., Farlora, R., Gallardo-Escárate, C. (2018). Whole-genome transcript expression profiling reveals novel insights into transposon genes and non-coding RNAs during Atlantic salmon seawater adaptation. *Scientific Reports*, 8, 2795.  
**DOI**: [10.1038/s41598-018-21039-2](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-018-21039-2)  
**URL**: [https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-018-21039-2](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-018-21039-2)  
**Summary**: Comprehensive transcriptome analysis revealing the role of non-coding RNAs in Atlantic salmon's adaptation to seawater. Identifies novel regulatory networks involving transposable elements and long non-coding RNAs in osmotic regulation.

### 9. Transgenerational Inheritance in Fish
**Citation**: Gapp, K., Jawaid, A., Sarkies, P., Bohacek, J., Pelczar, P., Prados, J., Farinelli, L., Miska, E., & Mansuy, I.M. (2014). Implication of sperm RNAs in transgenerational inheritance of the effects of early trauma in mice. *Nature Neuroscience*, 17(5), 667-669.  
**DOI**: [10.1038/nn.3695](https://doi.org/10.1038/nn.3695)  
**URL**: [https://www.nature.com/articles/nn.3695](https://www.nature.com/articles/nn.3695)  
**Summary**: Landmark study demonstrating that sperm RNAs can mediate transgenerational inheritance of stress responses. While conducted in mice, this paper established fundamental mechanisms relevant to understanding transgenerational effects in all vertebrates, including marine species.

## Week 7-8: Climate Change and Population Variation

### 10. Ocean Warming and Epigenetic Adaptation
**Citation**: Putnam, H.M., Davidson, J.M., & Gates, R.D. (2016). Ocean acidification influences host DNA methylation and phenotypic plasticity in environmentally susceptible corals. *Evolutionary Applications*, 9(9), 1165-1178.  
**DOI**: [10.1111/eva.12408](https://doi.org/10.1111/eva.12408)  
**URL**: [https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/eva.12408](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/eva.12408)  
**Summary**: Demonstrates how ocean acidification directly affects DNA methylation patterns in corals, leading to phenotypic changes. Shows mechanistic links between environmental change and epigenetic responses, with implications for coral adaptation to climate change.

### 11. Population Epigenomics in Marine Species
**Citation**: Schunter, C., Welch, M.J., Ryu, T., Zhang, H., Berumen, M.L., Nilsson, G.E., Munday, P.L., & Ravasi, T. (2016). Molecular signatures of transgenerational response to ocean acidification in a species of reef fish. *Nature Climate Change*, 6(11), 1014-1018.  
**DOI**: [10.1038/nclimate3087](https://doi.org/10.1038/nclimate3087)  
**URL**: [https://www.nature.com/articles/nclimate3087](https://www.nature.com/articles/nclimate3087)  
**Summary**: First evidence of transgenerational acclimation to ocean acidification in reef fish. Shows that parental exposure to elevated CO2 leads to altered gene expression in offspring through epigenetic mechanisms, demonstrating rapid adaptation potential.

### 12. Geographic Variation in Methylation
**Citation**: Baldanzi, S., Watson, R., McQuaid, C.D., Gouws, G., & Porri, F. (2017). Epigenetic variation among natural populations of the South African sandhopper *Talorchestia capensis*. *Evolutionary Ecology*, 31(1), 77-91.  
**DOI**: [10.1007/s10682-016-9877-9](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10682-016-9877-9)  
**URL**: [https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10682-016-9877-9](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10682-016-9877-9)  
**Summary**: Examines geographic patterns of epigenetic variation in marine invertebrates. Demonstrates that DNA methylation patterns vary among populations in ways that correlate with environmental gradients, suggesting local epigenetic adaptation.

## Week 9: Methodology and Techniques

### 13. Bisulfite Sequencing in Marine Organisms
**Citation**: Olson, C.E. & Roberts, S.B. (2014). Genome-wide profiling of DNA methylation and gene expression in *Crassostrea gigas* male gametes. *Frontiers in Physiology*, 5, 224.  
**DOI**: [10.3389/fphys.2014.00224](https://doi.org/10.3389/fphys.2014.00224)  
**URL**: [https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fphys.2014.00224/full](https://www.frontiersin.org/articles/10.3389/fphys.2014.00224/full)  
**Summary**: Comprehensive methodology paper describing whole-genome bisulfite sequencing approaches in Pacific oyster gametes. Provides detailed protocols and analytical approaches for studying DNA methylation in marine molluscs with limited genomic resources.

### 14. ChIP-seq in Marine Species
**Citation**: Rivière, G., Wu, G.C., Fellous, A., Goux, D., Sourdaine, P., & Favrel, P. (2013). DNA methylation is crucial for the early development in the oyster *C. gigas*. *Marine Biotechnology*, 15(6), 739-753.  
**DOI**: [10.1007/s10126-013-9523-2](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10126-013-9523-2)  
**URL**: [https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10126-013-9523-2](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10126-013-9523-2)  
**Summary**: Demonstrates the critical role of DNA methylation in oyster development using combined molecular and pharmacological approaches. Provides methodological insights for studying epigenetic regulation during marine invertebrate development.

### 15. Single-cell Epigenomics Approaches
**Citation**: Clark, S.J., Argelaguet, R., Kapourani, C.A., Stubbs, T.M., Lee, H.J., Alda-Catalinas, C., Krueger, F., Sanguinetti, G., Kelsey, G., Marioni, J.C., Stegle, O., & Reik, W. (2018). scNMT-seq enables joint profiling of chromatin accessibility, DNA methylation and transcription in single cells. *Nature Communications*, 9, 781.  
**DOI**: [10.1038/s41467-018-03149-4](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41467-018-03149-4)  
**URL**: [https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-03149-4](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-03149-4)  
**Summary**: Groundbreaking methodology paper introducing single-cell multi-omics approaches for studying epigenetic regulation. While developed in mammalian systems, these approaches are increasingly being adapted for marine organism research.

## Week 10: Current Frontiers and Future Directions

### 16. CRISPR Epigenome Editing
**Citation**: Vojta, A., Dobrinić, P., Tadić, V., Bočkor, L., Korać, P., Julg, B., Klasić, M., & Zoldoš, V. (2016). Repurposing the CRISPR-Cas9 system for targeted DNA methylation. *Nucleic Acids Research*, 44(12), 5615-5628.  
**DOI**: [10.1093/nar/gkw159](https://doi.org/10.1093/nar/gkw159)  
**URL**: [https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/44/12/5615/2457750](https://academic.oup.com/nar/article/44/12/5615/2457750)  
**Summary**: Describes novel CRISPR-based approaches for targeted DNA methylation editing. Represents cutting-edge technology that will likely be applied to marine organisms for functional studies of epigenetic regulation.

### 17. Environmental Epigenomics and Restoration
**Citation**: Torda, G., Donelson, J.M., Aranda, M., Barshis, D.J., Bay, L., Berumen, M.L., Bourne, D.G., Cantin, N., Foret, S., Matz, M., Miller, D.J., Moya, A., Putnam, H.M., Ravasi, T., van Oppen, M.J.H., Thurber, R.V., Vidal-Dupiol, J., Voolstra, C.R., Watson, S., Whitelaw, E., Willis, B.L., & Munday, P.L. (2017). Rapid adaptive responses to climate change in corals. *Nature Climate Change*, 7(9), 627-636.  
**DOI**: [10.1038/nclimate3374](https://doi.org/10.1038/nclimate3374)  
**URL**: [https://www.nature.com/articles/nclimate3374](https://www.nature.com/articles/nclimate3374)  
**Summary**: Comprehensive review of rapid adaptation mechanisms in corals, emphasizing the role of epigenetic processes. Discusses implications for coral restoration and management in the context of climate change.

### 18. Microbiome-Epigenome Interactions
**Citation**: Webster, N.S., Negri, A.P., Botté, E.S., Laffy, P.W., Flores, F., Noonan, S., Schmidt, C., & Uthicke, S. (2016). Host-associated coral reef microbes respond to the cumulative pressures of ocean warming and ocean acidification. *Scientific Reports*, 6, 19324.  
**DOI**: [10.1038/srep19324](https://doi.org/10.1038/srep19324)  
**URL**: [https://www.nature.com/articles/srep19324](https://www.nature.com/articles/srep19324)  
**Summary**: Examines how coral-associated microbial communities respond to climate stressors, with implications for host-microbe epigenetic interactions. Represents emerging frontier in understanding multi-organism epigenetic systems.

### 19. Machine Learning in Epigenomics
**Citation**: Angermueller, C., Clark, S.J., Lee, H.J., Macaulay, I.C., Teng, M.J., Hu, T.X., Krueger, F., Smallwood, S.A., Ponting, C.P., Voet, T., Kelsey, G., Stegle, O., & Reik, W. (2016). Parallel single-cell sequencing links transcriptional and epigenetic heterogeneity. *Nature Methods*, 13(3), 229-232.  
**DOI**: [10.1038/nmeth.3728](https://doi.org/10.1038/nmeth.3728)  
**URL**: [https://www.nature.com/articles/nmeth.3728](https://www.nature.com/articles/nmeth.3728)  
**Summary**: Introduces computational methods for analyzing single-cell multi-omics data, including epigenetic information. Represents the future of high-resolution epigenetic analysis that will be crucial for understanding cellular heterogeneity in marine organisms.

### 20. Epigenetic Biomarkers for Environmental Health
**Citation**: Beal, A.P., Ramos-Madrigal, J., Dias, G., Ackiss, A.S., Endo, M., Harvey, E.S., Reimer, J.D., & Paulay, G. (2018). The promises and pitfalls of museum genomics. *Molecular Ecology*, 27(21), 4759-4769.  
**DOI**: [10.1111/mec.14885](https://doi.org/10.1111/mec.14885)  
**URL**: [https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/mec.14885](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/abs/10.1111/mec.14885)  
**Summary**: Discusses the potential and challenges of using museum specimens for genomic and epigenomic research. Highlights the importance of historical samples for understanding long-term environmental changes and their epigenetic consequences in marine organisms.
